Human-Centered Green Tech

Interventions for Encouraging Sustainable Behavior

Global CO2 emissions are on the rise with the compounding effects of human activities related to transportation, agriculture, or energy production and usage [3]. While there is a need for large corporations and policymakers to play their part in curbing GHG (Greenhouse Gas) emissions [2, 8], individual consumers can also play a big role in reducing their carbon footprints by making eco-friendly choices in their daily lives [5, 15]. Previous work has shown the potential of persuasive eco-feedback technologies (i.e., technologies that provide feedback on individual or group behaviors with a goal of reducing environmental impact) in aiding users to make sustainable choices across a wide range of domains, including food consumption [13, 18], household energy usage [6, 17], transit [4, 10], and waste disposal [7]. However, there still remains a wide gap in the widespread adoption of these technologies. As smart devices increasingly become part of our everyday lives, they present interesting opportunities for researchers to identify potential areas of human-AI interaction that can be leveraged for encouraging green behavior amongst users.

With the advent of IoT (Internet-of-Things) devices, there is a huge potential for leveraging sensory data to study user behavior, derive insights using ML algorithms, and introduce interventions backed by behavioral science for encouraging sustainable behavior [11]. Large-scale analysis of interaction data can not only help us understand desirable green behavioral patterns amongst users [9], but also identify potential areas for nudging users to make a change. Behavioral science theories and HCI concepts can help inform the design of different interventions for encouraging users to make eco-friendly choices [12]. AI models can help support these eco-feedback efforts in a significant manner, be it through dynamic interventions, personalized notification delivery [16], counterfactual environmental impact of eco-friendly user actions [14], etc. Through this workshop, we will explore these different human-AI interaction design avenues to not only promote eco-friendly decision making, but also foster carbon literacy through a “learning by doing” approach.

Call for Participation

As smart technologies continue to proliferate in different spheres of our daily lives [1], they also present interesting opportunities and challenges for fostering green user behavior. The goal of this workshop is to understand user behavior around technology, derive insights, and design \textbf{human-centered green technology interventions for encouraging sustainable user behavior}. While industry leaders and policymakers strive towards making efforts to reduce ever-increasing global greenhouse gas emissions [2, 3, 8], we aim to look beyond the "let's make greener buildings and cars" notion in this workshop. Instead, we focus on the individuals who can play a big role in reducing their carbon footprints by making eco-friendly choices in their daily lives [5, 12, 15].

We ask the following questions:

  • What technologies can we design to support greener interactions and behavior?
  • What kind of datasets can researchers generate and study for understanding user attitudes and eco-friendly behavior?
  • How do people interpret CO2 emissions?
  • What motivates people to be more eco-aware?
  • What are some of the challenges one might encounter in the design and adoption of green technologies?

We invite submissions of all forms, but not limited to:

  • Position statements and papers (2-4 pages)
  • Slide (Powerpoint/Keynote/etc.) Decks
  • Video demos
  • Hack ideas

This one-day workshop aims to call on CHI researchers working in a wide range of domains including: behavioral science, human-centered design, human-AI interaction, climate change, sustainable HCI, and others to propose novel design interventions to encourage eco-friendly user behavior. The application domains include but are not limited to transportation, food consumption, home energy usage, and smart workplaces. Submissions will be collected via EasyChair. Please direct questions to humancenteredgreentech@gmail.com.

Organizers

Vikram Mohanty is a PhD candidate in the Department of Computer Science at Virginia Tech. His research focuses on human-AI interaction and crowdsourcing, specifically on developing and studying novel tools that leverage the complementary strengths of AI and collective human intelligence for solving complex, open-ended problems. As part of his research, he built Civil War Photo Sleuth, an online platform that combines crowdsourced human expertise and facial recognition to identify unknown portraits from the American Civil War era. His work has received major press coverage, including Smithsonian, Time, American Way, The History Channel, and Popular Mechanics, and won the Microsoft Cloud AI Research Challenge Grand Prize (2018), the ACM IUI Best Paper (2019) award and the AAAI HCOMP Best Poster/Demo (2018) award. Vikram has worked as a research intern at VSCO, Dataminr, and Toyota Research Institute (TRI). Prior to his PhD, he completed his undergraduate from the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT), Kharagpur.

Jaeyoon Song is a PhD student at MIT Sloan in the Information Technologies (IT) group - a subgroup of the Management Science program. Her general research area is social computing and computational social science at the intersection of computer science, data science, and social science. She employs data-driven computational thinking and methods to explore social science questions. Specifically, She is interested in human-AI collaboration, online discussions/meetings, and social media.

Rie Kamikubo is a Ph.D. candidate in the College of Information Studies at the University of Maryland, College Park. Her research focuses on Human Computer Interaction (HCI) and accessibility, specifically on the design and evaluation of inclusive technologies for people with disabilities. She obtained her bachelor's degree in Cognitive Science specialized in HCI from the University of California, San Diego and her master's degree in Information Studies at the University of Tokyo. Prior to entering the PhD program, she worked at the Robotics Institute, Carnegie Mellon University on designing navigation systems for blind people.

Dr. Hamed Alavi is a tenure track assistant professor at the University of Amsterdam. His research is focused on the future of human's interactive experiences with built environments. Particularly, he is interested in the engagement of computer science in the evolution of buildings and urban spaces as they increasingly incorporate artificial intelligence, context-aware automation, and interactivity.

Dr. David Ayman Shamma is a distinguished industry scientist researching people, media experiences, and artificial intelligence. At Toyota Research Institute, his work is focused on understanding drivers to improve carbon literacy through interactive experiences. He has worked on Edge AI, cameras, and future of work at FX Palo Alto Laboratory, AI sensors for wearable fashion at Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica (CWI), HCI + AI as a director of research at Yahoo Labs/Flickr, and UXR for remote knowledge sharing at NASA's Center for Mars Exploration. He is a Distinguished Member of the ACM, is a Senior Member of IEEE, and holds over 25 patents. His research attracts international media attention, including Wired, The New York Times, and the Library of Congress. He was named a Master Inventor at Yahoo and his research at CWI received an European Design Award. He recently was a Technical Program Chair for the ACM CHI 2022 conference and previously was a VP on the ACM SIGCHI Executive Committee. Ayman holds a Ph.D. in Computer Science from Northwestern University and completed his M.S./B.S. at the IHMC at UWF.

Dr. Charlene C. Wu is a behavioral science leader bridging academic and technology sectors to advance research in human-centered AI. She is currently the Director of the Human-Centered AI (HCAI) division at the Toyota Research Institute (TRI). The HCAI division aims to develop AI systems to augment human decision-making by leveraging research at the intersection between behavioral science, machine learning, and human-computer interaction. Before TRI, Charlene founded the applied behavioral data science disciplines at Uber and Airbnb, where her team applied behavioral science research and methodologies to generate novel hypotheses and prototype solutions to guide global business strategy. Charlene is passionate about discovering and pursuing novel opportunities where behavioral science can have an outsized impact. Charlene has been a speaker at the World Economic Forum, Behavioral Science Summit, and Women in Analytics conferences and serves as a mentor for Data Science for All and a strategic advisor for both early-stage startups and publicly traded companies. She holds Bachelor's degrees in economics and psychology from UCLA and Master's and Ph.D. degrees in psychology from Stanford University.
